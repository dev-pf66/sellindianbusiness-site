## Introduction

You've built a great business. But buyers see it differently than you do.

Where you see years of hard work, buyers see numbers. Where you see loyal customers, buyers see concentration risk. Where you see yourself, buyers see key person dependency.

Understanding what buyers want—and how they evaluate businesses—is the key to commanding a premium price.

This guide takes you inside the buyer's mind. You'll learn:
- The top criteria buyers evaluate
- Red flags that kill deals
- How different buyer types think
- Specific ways to make your business more attractive

**Think like a buyer. Price like a seller.**

## Section 1: The Top 10 Things Buyers Look For

### 1. Clean, Accurate Financials

**What buyers want:**
- Audited or reviewed financial statements
- Clear separation of business and personal expenses
- Accurate records going back 3+ years
- Financials that match tax returns
- Quick, confident answers to financial questions

**Red flags:**
- Multiple sets of books
- Significant cash transactions
- Owner's personal expenses mixed in
- Unable to produce requested documents
- Numbers that don't reconcile

**Why it matters:**
Financials are how buyers determine value. If they can't trust your numbers, they either walk away or discount heavily for uncertainty.

**Insider tip:** Create a "quality of earnings" summary—a clean document showing your adjusted EBITDA with all add-backs clearly explained.

---

### 2. Consistent Revenue and Profit Growth

**What buyers want:**
- Revenue growth of 10-20%+ annually
- Stable or improving profit margins
- Predictable, repeatable revenue patterns
- No major unexplained fluctuations
- Positive trajectory into the future

**Red flags:**
- Declining revenue
- Shrinking margins
- Erratic year-over-year changes
- Heavy dependence on one-time deals
- Recent sharp drops in any metric

**Why it matters:**
Buyers are buying future cash flows. Growth demonstrates the business is healthy and has runway. Decline suggests problems ahead.

**Insider tip:** If you have a bad year, be ready to explain it clearly and show recovery. Context matters.

---

### 3. Recurring or Repeatable Revenue

**What buyers want:**
- Subscription or contract-based revenue
- Long-term customer relationships
- High customer retention rates
- Predictable monthly/quarterly patterns
- Automatic renewals

**Revenue quality hierarchy (best to worst):**
1. Contractual recurring (subscriptions, retainers)
2. Recurring by habit (repeat customers)
3. Re-occurring (seasonal, project-based repeats)
4. Non-recurring (one-time sales)

**Why it matters:**
Recurring revenue reduces risk. A buyer knows cash will continue flowing after acquisition. One-time revenue requires constant re-selling.

**Insider tip:** If you don't have formal subscriptions, document your repeat customer patterns. Show that X% of customers return annually.

---

### 4. Diversified Customer Base

**What buyers want:**
- No single customer >15% of revenue
- Top 10 customers <50% of revenue
- Multiple customer segments
- Geographic diversity
- Mix of customer sizes

**Red flags:**
- One customer >30% of revenue
- Top 3 customers = majority of business
- Single industry exposure
- All customers from one region
- Heavy government/tender dependence

**Why it matters:**
Customer concentration = risk concentration. If your biggest customer leaves, how badly does the business suffer?

**Insider tip:** If you have concentration, document the relationship history, contractual protections, and any multi-year commitments. Show why they're sticky.

---

### 5. Business Runs Without the Owner

**What buyers want:**
- Strong management team in place
- Documented systems and processes
- Key relationships held by the business, not the owner
- Owner not involved in daily operations
- Business maintained performance during owner's absences

**Red flags:**
- Owner handles all sales
- Key customers only work with owner
- No second-in-command
- No written procedures
- Owner hasn't taken a real vacation in years

**Why it matters:**
Buyers aren't buying you—they're buying a business. If you're essential, they're buying a job and a risk.

**Insider tip:** Before going to market, take a 2-week vacation. Don't check in. See what breaks. Fix those things.

---

### 6. Defensible Market Position

**What buyers want:**
- Clear competitive advantages
- High barriers to entry
- Strong brand recognition
- Proprietary technology or processes
- Exclusive relationships or territories

**Types of defensibility:**
- **Brand:** Customers choose you by name
- **Technology:** Patents, proprietary systems
- **Relationships:** Exclusive contracts, switching costs
- **Scale:** Cost advantages competitors can't match
- **Network effects:** More users = more valuable
- **Regulatory:** Licenses competitors can't get

**Red flags:**
- Commodity business
- Easy to replicate
- Competing purely on price
- No barriers to new entrants

**Why it matters:**
Defensibility = sustainability. Buyers want to know competitors won't erode profits immediately after acquisition.

**Insider tip:** Document your competitive moat clearly. Even small advantages matter when articulated well.

---

### 7. Growth Potential

**What buyers want:**
- Clear expansion opportunities
- Untapped market segments
- New product/service possibilities
- Geographic expansion potential
- Demonstrated but not yet realized growth

**Examples:**
- "We've only scratched the surface in South India"
- "Our manufacturing capacity utilization is only 60%"
- "We've been turning away B2B inquiries to focus on B2C"
- "Competitors in other regions are 3x our size"

**Red flags:**
- Market is saturated
- No obvious expansion path
- Already at full capacity with no investment plan
- Industry is declining

**Why it matters:**
Buyers pay for future value, not just current earnings. Growth potential justifies premium multiples.

**Insider tip:** Be specific about growth opportunities. Vague "huge market" claims don't convince anyone. Numbers and plans do.

---

### 8. Quality Team and Culture

**What buyers want:**
- Experienced, stable management team
- Low employee turnover
- Documented roles and responsibilities
- Competitive compensation structure
- Positive workplace culture
- Key employees willing to stay post-acquisition

**Red flags:**
- High turnover
- Key positions unfilled
- No formal HR processes
- Compensation below market
- Pending employee disputes
- Critical staff planning to leave

**Why it matters:**
Employees are often the business's most valuable asset. Buyers need confidence the team will stay.

**Insider tip:** Identify key employees and have a retention plan. Buyers often require key person retention agreements as a condition of closing.

---

### 9. Clean Legal and Compliance

**What buyers want:**
- No pending litigation
- Full regulatory compliance
- Clean environmental record
- Proper corporate structure
- All licenses and permits current
- Intellectual property properly protected

**Red flags:**
- Ongoing lawsuits
- Regulatory violations
- Environmental issues
- Corporate record gaps
- Expired licenses
- IP ownership questions

**Why it matters:**
Legal issues can blow up deals entirely or create massive price reductions. No buyer wants hidden liabilities.

**Insider tip:** Address issues before going to market. Disclose what can't be fixed—surprises in due diligence kill trust.

---

### 10. Reasonable Valuation Expectations

**What buyers want:**
- Asking price supported by financials
- Understanding of market multiples
- Flexibility on deal structure
- Realistic view of business strengths and weaknesses
- Willingness to negotiate fairly

**Red flags:**
- Price disconnected from earnings
- "I need X amount" as justification
- Comparing to unrelated businesses
- Unwillingness to discuss price
- Emotional attachment inflating expectations

**Why it matters:**
Unrealistic pricing wastes everyone's time. Buyers pass on overpriced businesses, even good ones.

**Insider tip:** Get a professional valuation before setting your price. Come to negotiations informed, not hopeful.

## Section 2: What Different Buyers Want

Not all buyers are the same. Understanding buyer types helps you position appropriately.

### Strategic Buyers

**Who they are:** Companies in your industry or adjacent industries.

**What they want:**
- Synergies with their existing business
- Customer base they can cross-sell to
- Geographic expansion
- Talent and capabilities
- Competitive elimination
- Supply chain integration

**What they pay:**
Premium multiples (often 20-50% above financial buyers) for the right strategic fit.

**How to attract them:**
- Emphasize synergy opportunities
- Show complementary capabilities
- Highlight what they can do with your business that you couldn't
- Be open to integration discussions

---

### Financial Buyers (PE/Family Offices)

**Who they are:** Investors looking for returns—private equity firms, family offices, high-net-worth individuals.

**What they want:**
- Strong, consistent cash flows
- Growth potential (they'll invest to grow)
- Professional management team
- Clear value creation opportunities
- Reasonable entry valuation
- Exit potential in 3-5 years

**What they pay:**
Fair market value based on earnings multiples. Less premium than strategic buyers.

**How to attract them:**
- Demonstrate predictable cash flows
- Show management can execute without you
- Present clear growth plan they can fund
- Be realistic on valuation

---

### Individual Buyers

**Who they are:** Entrepreneurs, executives seeking to own a business, first-time buyers.

**What they want:**
- Business they can understand and operate
- Manageable size (typically smaller deals)
- Lifestyle considerations (location, hours)
- Seller willing to train and transition
- Financing support or seller financing
- Lower risk profile

**What they pay:**
Often lower multiples, but willing to pay for quality businesses.

**How to attract them:**
- Demonstrate the business is learnable
- Offer training and transition support
- Be open to seller financing
- Show lifestyle benefits
- Highlight existing systems and processes

---

### Industry Consolidators

**Who they are:** Larger companies or PE-backed platforms buying multiple businesses in your sector.

**What they want:**
- Tuck-in acquisition opportunities
- Efficiency gains through consolidation
- Local market presence
- Customer relationships
- Experienced staff
- Proven operational models

**What they pay:**
Varies widely. Can pay premium for strategic fits or discount for "diamond in the rough" situations.

**How to attract them:**
- Understand the consolidation landscape in your industry
- Position as a platform builder or strong tuck-in
- Emphasize local market strength
- Show operational best practices they can replicate

## Section 3: Red Flags That Kill Deals

These issues cause buyers to either walk away or dramatically reduce their offers:

### Deal Killers (Buyers Walk Away)

1. **Fraud or material misrepresentation**
2. **Owner unwilling to provide information**
3. **Major undisclosed litigation**
4. **Environmental contamination**
5. **Serious regulatory violations**
6. **Financial statements that don't hold up**
7. **Key customer already leaving**
8. **Seller becomes adversarial**

### Price Killers (Major Discounts)

1. **Customer concentration >40%**
2. **Significant owner dependency**
3. **Declining revenue trend**
4. **Large working capital requirements**
5. **Upcoming major capital expenditures**
6. **Key employee flight risk**
7. **Legal issues that transfer with sale**
8. **Below-market lease expiring**

### Deal Delayers (Slow or Complicated Closings)

1. **Missing documentation**
2. **Complex corporate structure**
3. **Third-party consents required**
4. **Inventory discrepancies**
5. **Unresolved tax issues**
6. **Lease transfer complications**
7. **Employee issues**
8. **Financing conditions**

## Section 4: How to Position Your Business for Premium Offers

### Create Your Investment Thesis

Answer these questions in one page:
1. What does your business do?
2. Why is it valuable?
3. What's the growth opportunity?
4. Why now?
5. Why would someone pay a premium?

### Prepare Your Story

**The narrative arc:**
1. **Origin:** How and why the business started
2. **Growth:** Key milestones and achievements
3. **Current:** What the business looks like today
4. **Future:** Where it could go with the right owner

### Address Weaknesses Proactively

Every business has weaknesses. Address them before buyers find them.

**Template:**
"We know [weakness] is a concern. Here's what we've done about it: [mitigation]. Here's the reality: [honest assessment]."

This builds trust and prevents surprises.

### Quantify Everything

Buyers speak numbers. Translate your qualitative strengths into quantitative evidence.

| Instead of... | Say... |
|---------------|--------|
| "Great customer relationships" | "92% retention rate, average customer tenure 4.7 years" |
| "Strong team" | "Average employee tenure 6 years, 8% annual turnover" |
| "Growing market" | "Industry growing 15% annually, our segment growing 22%" |
| "Good margins" | "Gross margin 45%, EBITDA margin 18%" |

### Create FOMO (Carefully)

Buyers respond to competition. If you have genuine interest from multiple parties, let them know.

**Do:** "We've received interest from several parties and will be running a structured process."

**Don't:** Manufacture false urgency or lie about other offers.

## Section 5: The Due Diligence Deep Dive

Here's what buyers examine most closely:

### Financial Due Diligence

**What they analyze:**
- Quality of earnings (are profits real and recurring?)
- Revenue by customer and product
- Gross margin trends
- Working capital requirements
- Capital expenditure needs
- Off-balance sheet items
- Related party transactions
- Cash vs. accrual differences

**Prepare by:** Having clear explanations for any anomalies and backup documentation for all numbers.

### Commercial Due Diligence

**What they analyze:**
- Market size and growth
- Competitive landscape
- Customer satisfaction
- Sales pipeline
- Pricing power
- Contract terms and renewals

**Prepare by:** Gathering market research, customer data, and competitive analysis.

### Operational Due Diligence

**What they analyze:**
- Facilities and equipment condition
- IT systems and security
- Supply chain resilience
- Quality control processes
- Production capacity
- Operational efficiency

**Prepare by:** Documenting processes and addressing obvious operational issues.

### Legal Due Diligence

**What they analyze:**
- Corporate records
- Material contracts
- Litigation history
- Compliance status
- Intellectual property
- Employment matters
- Real estate

**Prepare by:** Organizing all legal documents and resolving outstanding issues.

## FAQ Section

**Q: What's the single most important thing buyers look for?**
A: Clean, accurate financials. Without trustworthy numbers, buyers can't assess value, and deals fall apart. Everything else becomes secondary if financials aren't credible.

**Q: How do I reduce owner dependency quickly?**
A: Start by documenting your key processes, delegating customer relationships to your team, and empowering a second-in-command to make decisions. Take a real vacation and don't check in—see what breaks.

**Q: Will buyers pay more for a business with a good story?**
A: Yes, within reason. A compelling narrative about why your business is special, how it grew, and where it can go helps buyers see potential. But story must be backed by numbers.

**Q: Should I hide problems from buyers?**
A: Never. Every issue comes out in due diligence. Hiding problems destroys trust and kills deals. Disclosing issues proactively—with context and mitigation—builds credibility.
