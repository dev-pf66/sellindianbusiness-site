You've spent months negotiating the sale of your business. The buyer likes what they see, but there's a catch: they're offering ₹10 crore upfront and another ₹5 crore as an "earnout"—contingent on the business hitting certain targets after you sell.

Should you accept?

Earnouts are increasingly common in Indian business sales, especially for SMEs where valuation is uncertain. They can bridge gaps between what sellers want and buyers are willing to pay upfront. But they're also a source of disputes and disappointment.

This guide explains how earnouts work, when they make sense, and how to protect yourself if you agree to one.

## What Is an Earnout?

An earnout is a portion of the purchase price that's paid after closing, contingent on the business achieving specified performance targets. It's "earn" + "out"—you earn your way out of the contingency.

**Simple example:**
- Purchase price: ₹15 crore total
- Upfront payment: ₹10 crore at closing
- Earnout: ₹5 crore if business achieves ₹8 crore revenue in Year 1

If the business hits ₹8 crore revenue, you get the additional ₹5 crore. If it doesn't, you get nothing (or a prorated amount, depending on the terms).

### Why Earnouts Exist

Earnouts solve a fundamental problem: **valuation disagreement**.

**Seller's view:** "My business is growing 30% annually. If you apply the same multiple to next year's profits, it's worth much more."

**Buyer's view:** "Growth projections are uncertain. I'll pay based on what's proven, not what's projected."

An earnout bridges this gap: the buyer pays today's value upfront, and the seller earns tomorrow's value if tomorrow actually delivers.

### Other Reasons for Earnouts

- **Information asymmetry:** Buyer can't fully verify seller's claims
- **Risk sharing:** Both parties share uncertainty about future performance
- **Financing constraints:** Buyer can't afford full price upfront
- **Regulatory uncertainty:** Value depends on pending regulatory decisions
- **Customer concentration:** Key customer relationships need to prove they'll stay
- **Integration risk:** Value depends on successful integration

## Common Earnout Structures

### Revenue-Based Earnouts

Payment tied to revenue targets:
- "₹2 crore if Year 1 revenue exceeds ₹10 crore"
- "1% of all revenue above ₹8 crore for 3 years"

**Advantages:**
- Simple to measure
- Hard to manipulate through accounting choices
- Seller can influence through sales efforts

**Disadvantages:**
- Buyer might sacrifice profitability for revenue
- Price cuts or bundling can inflate revenue
- Doesn't capture profitability improvements

### Profit-Based Earnouts (EBITDA, Net Profit)

Payment tied to profitability:
- "₹3 crore if EBITDA exceeds ₹2 crore in Year 1"
- "50% of EBITDA above ₹1.5 crore for 2 years"

**Advantages:**
- Measures true business value
- Aligns seller and buyer interests
- Captures efficiency improvements

**Disadvantages:**
- Easily manipulated through expense allocation
- Accounting policies affect calculation
- Buyer controls many profit-affecting decisions

### Milestone-Based Earnouts

Payment tied to specific events:
- "₹1 crore upon renewal of contract with Customer X"
- "₹50 lakh per new city launched, up to ₹2.5 crore"
- "₹2 crore if regulatory approval obtained within 12 months"

**Advantages:**
- Clear, binary triggers
- Easy to verify
- Matches payment to specific value drivers

**Disadvantages:**
- May not capture overall performance
- Some milestones are outside anyone's control
- Narrow focus can distort priorities

### Hybrid Structures

Combinations of the above:
- "₹2 crore if revenue exceeds ₹10 crore AND EBITDA margin exceeds 15%"
- "Revenue-based earnout in Year 1, profit-based in Year 2"
- "Minimum ₹1 crore guaranteed, plus performance upside"

## The Risks of Earnouts for Sellers

### Risk 1: Losing Control

After closing, the buyer controls:
- Pricing decisions
- Marketing budgets
- Hiring and firing
- Capital investments
- Strategic direction

Any of these can tank your earnout. A buyer might:
- Cut marketing spend (saves money now, hurts revenue)
- Raise prices (improves margins, loses volume)
- Divert customers to other business units
- Allocate corporate overhead that kills EBITDA

**You have responsibility without authority.**

### Risk 2: Accounting Manipulation

For profit-based earnouts, the buyer controls accounting:
- Recognizing revenue earlier or later
- Accelerating or deferring expenses
- Allocating overhead from other businesses
- Transfer pricing with related entities
- Reserve and provision decisions

Even "GAAP" or "consistent with historical practices" leaves room for discretion.

### Risk 3: Disputes and Litigation

Earnout agreements are among the most disputed provisions in M&A. Common fights include:
- How to calculate the metric
- What expenses to include
- What constitutes "ordinary course"
- Who caused a missed target
- What information seller can access

Litigation is expensive, time-consuming, and uncertain.

### Risk 4: Never Getting Paid

Industry data suggests that 30-50% of earnouts never pay out fully. Reasons include:
- Legitimate underperformance
- Buyer manipulation
- Integration disruption
- Market changes
- Ambiguous contract terms

That "₹5 crore earnout" may realistically be worth ₹2-3 crore expected value.

### Risk 5: Taxation Uncertainty

In India, the tax treatment of earnouts isn't always clear:
- Is it capital gains (when accrued) or additional consideration (when received)?
- What if you never receive it?
- Can you claim loss if earnout isn't paid?

Work with a CA to understand the implications for your specific structure.

## When to Consider Accepting an Earnout

Despite the risks, earnouts can make sense:

### 1. You're Staying Involved

If you'll continue managing the business post-sale (as employee or consultant), you have some control over results. You can influence whether targets are met.

### 2. The Upfront Amount Is Acceptable

If the guaranteed portion (upfront payment) is fair value for today's business, the earnout is pure upside. Ask yourself: "Would I sell for just the upfront amount?" If yes, the earnout is a bonus.

### 3. You Have Confidence in the Projections

If the business is genuinely on a growth trajectory and targets are achievable even with conservative assumptions, the earnout aligns your interests with reality.

### 4. No Better Alternative Exists

Sometimes the market won't pay your desired price upfront. An earnout may be the only path to achieving your target valuation.

### 5. Specific Uncertainty Exists

If there's a specific event that will determine value (regulatory approval, key customer renewal), a milestone earnout can be fair.

## When to Resist an Earnout

### 1. You're Completely Exiting

If you're walking away at closing, you'll have no ability to influence results. This is the worst scenario for earnouts.

### 2. The Buyer Has a Track Record of Disputes

Research the buyer. Have they been involved in earnout litigation? Do they have a reputation for squeezing sellers?

### 3. The Metrics Are Easily Manipulated

Profit-based earnouts with vague "ordinary course" standards are dangerous. If you can't lock down the calculations, don't accept.

### 4. Too Much Value Is Contingent

If the earnout is more than 30-40% of total consideration, you're taking significant risk. Push for more upfront.

### 5. The Period Is Too Long

Two-year earnouts are standard. Three years is stretching it. Beyond that, too much can change.

## How to Negotiate a Fair Earnout

If you're accepting an earnout, negotiate protections:

### 1. Maximize the Upfront Payment

The most important negotiation is the split between upfront and earnout. Push for as much guaranteed payment as possible.

**Tactic:** "I'm confident in the projections, so you should be too. If you believe in the growth, pay for it now."

### 2. Choose the Right Metric

**Revenue** is harder to manipulate than profit. If accepting profit-based earnout:
- Define EBITDA precisely
- List specific exclusions and inclusions
- Set caps on overhead allocation
- Require accounting policy consistency

### 3. Demand Operating Covenants

Require the buyer to operate the business in ways that give the earnout a fair chance:

- "Buyer shall operate the Business in the ordinary course consistent with past practice"
- "Buyer shall maintain marketing spend at least equal to ₹X lakh annually"
- "Buyer shall not divert customers to other Buyer businesses"
- "Buyer shall employ at least X salespeople"
- "Buyer shall not increase prices by more than X% without Seller consent"

### 4. Get Acceleration Triggers

Include provisions for early/automatic earnout payment:

- **Change of control:** If buyer sells the business, earnout accelerates at maximum
- **Breach:** If buyer materially breaches operating covenants, earnout becomes payable
- **Material changes:** If buyer fundamentally changes the business, earnout accelerates

### 5. Secure Information Rights

You can't evaluate what you can't see:

- Monthly/quarterly financial reports
- Annual audit rights
- Access to underlying books and records
- Right to ask questions and get explanations

### 6. Establish Dispute Resolution

Define a clear process for disagreements:

- Expert determination (faster than arbitration/litigation)
- Specific rules for the expert (accounting qualifications, timeline, binding decision)
- Clear scope of what can be disputed
- Cost allocation

### 7. Include a Floor/Threshold

Negotiate minimum payments:

- "Seller receives minimum ₹1 crore earnout regardless of performance"
- "Earnout vests pro-rata: 80% of target = 80% of earnout"
- "Partial credit for partial achievement"

### 8. Consider Escrow for Earnout

If there's any concern about buyer's ability to pay:

- Require earnout amount to be escrowed
- Or secured by guarantee or pledge
- Or paid by letter of credit

## Structuring Your Earnout: Examples

### Example 1: Revenue-Based (Seller-Friendly)

**Structure:**
- Earnout Period: 2 years
- Metric: Revenue
- Target: ₹10 crore Year 1, ₹12 crore Year 2
- Payment: ₹1 crore for achieving each year's target
- Pro-rata: Payment scales linearly from 80% to 100% of target
- Floor: ₹50 lakh per year if revenue exceeds 70% of target

**Why it works:**
- Revenue is hard to manipulate
- Pro-rata payment reduces all-or-nothing risk
- Floor guarantees minimum payment
- Two-year period is reasonable

### Example 2: EBITDA-Based (Balanced)

**Structure:**
- Earnout Period: 2 years
- Metric: Adjusted EBITDA (specifically defined)
- Target: ₹2 crore Year 1, ₹2.4 crore Year 2
- Payment: 2x EBITDA above ₹1.5 crore threshold, capped at ₹3 crore total
- Protections:
  - Marketing spend minimum of ₹30 lakh/year
  - No overhead allocation above ₹10 lakh/year
  - Accounting consistent with pre-closing practices
  - Seller has monthly reporting rights

**Why it works:**
- EBITDA aligns with value creation
- Threshold means earnout only for outperformance
- Cap limits buyer's exposure
- Protections limit manipulation

### Example 3: Milestone-Based

**Structure:**
- Milestone 1: ₹50 lakh upon renewal of ABC Corp contract (within 12 months)
- Milestone 2: ₹75 lakh upon regulatory approval for new product (within 18 months)
- Milestone 3: ₹1 crore upon achieving 1,000 monthly active users (within 24 months)

**Why it works:**
- Clear, verifiable milestones
- Tied to specific value drivers
- Different timelines for different events
- Binary nature reduces disputes

## Tax Treatment of Earnouts in India

### General Principles

Earnouts are typically treated as part of the sale consideration:

**If structured as additional purchase price:**
- Capital gains calculated when earnout is "ascertainable"
- Tax may be due even before earnout is received (contentious area)
- If earnout never materializes, amended return may be needed

**If structured as compensation for services:**
- Taxed as income when received
- Higher rates than capital gains
- May attract TDS

### Planning Considerations

**For Sellers:**
- Structure to defer tax until earnout is actually received
- Consider whether earnout can be structured as contingent consideration (recognized on receipt)
- Work with CA to optimize structure

**Documentation:**
- Purchase agreement should clearly characterize earnout
- "Contingent consideration" vs. "service compensation" has different implications
- Transfer pricing considerations if buyer is foreign

### Recent Developments

Tax authorities and courts have increasingly scrutinized earnout structures. Key issues:
- Whether to value earnout at signing (discounted) or payment
- Treatment of earnouts that never pay out
- GST implications if earnout is treated as services

**Recommendation:** Get specific tax advice before finalizing earnout structure.

## Real-World Earnout Disputes

### Case Study 1: The Diverted Customers

A software company was sold with a 2-year revenue-based earnout. After closing, the buyer began directing customers to its other products, citing "strategic portfolio management." Revenue missed targets, and no earnout was paid.

**Lesson:** Include explicit anti-diversion provisions: "Buyer shall not actively market or sell competing products to existing customers during the Earnout Period."

### Case Study 2: The Overhead Allocation

A manufacturing business was sold with an EBITDA-based earnout. Post-closing, the buyer allocated ₹80 lakh of "corporate overhead" to the business—HR, IT, and management services. EBITDA was crushed, and the earnout was zero.

**Lesson:** Cap overhead allocations specifically: "Corporate overhead allocated to the Business shall not exceed ₹15 lakh per year."

### Case Study 3: The Integration

A distributor sold to a strategic buyer who promised to "accelerate growth." Instead, they merged operations, disrupted relationships, and lost key employees. Revenue dropped 20%.

**Lesson:** Define what "ordinary course" means, and include specific operating covenants. Or accelerate earnout if buyer makes material changes.

### Case Study 4: The Success

A D2C brand was sold with a revenue-based earnout. The founder stayed on, had clear targets, received monthly reports, and worked collaboratively with the buyer. Earnout targets were exceeded, and full payment was made.

**Lesson:** Earnouts work when seller stays involved, metrics are clear, and both parties operate in good faith.

## Alternatives to Earnouts

If you're uncomfortable with earnouts, consider:

### 1. Lower Upfront Price with No Earnout

Accept less certain money now rather than uncertain money later.

### 2. Seller Financing

Instead of earnout: provide seller financing at interest. You get paid regardless of performance, with the note secured by business assets.

### 3. Retained Equity

Keep a minority stake (10-25%) instead of earnout. You share in upside directly rather than through a formula.

### 4. Consulting Agreement

Separate consulting payments for your continued involvement, not contingent on performance.

### 5. Extended Payment Period

Fixed payments over time, not contingent on metrics. Lower risk of non-payment.

## Checklist: Evaluating an Earnout Proposal

Before accepting, verify:

**Structure:**
- [ ] Is the upfront amount acceptable standalone?
- [ ] Is the earnout period reasonable (ideally ≤2 years)?
- [ ] Is the earnout a reasonable % of total consideration (<30%)?
- [ ] Are targets achievable under conservative assumptions?

**Metrics:**
- [ ] Is the metric clearly defined (revenue vs. EBITDA vs. milestone)?
- [ ] Is calculation methodology specified in detail?
- [ ] Are exclusions and inclusions listed?
- [ ] Is there consistency with historical practices?

**Control:**
- [ ] Will you remain involved in the business?
- [ ] Are there operating covenants protecting the earnout?
- [ ] Can you influence whether targets are met?

**Protection:**
- [ ] Is there a floor or minimum payment?
- [ ] Are there acceleration triggers?
- [ ] Do you have information/audit rights?
- [ ] Is there a clear dispute resolution mechanism?

**Payment:**
- [ ] Is buyer creditworthy?
- [ ] Is earnout secured or escrowed?
- [ ] Are payment timing and method specified?
