## Introduction

"What's my business worth?"

It's the question every seller asks first. And it's more complicated than you might think.

Your business isn't worth what you *think* it's worth. It's not worth what you *need* it to be worth. It's worth what a buyer will actually pay—based on financial performance, market conditions, and negotiation.

This guide will teach you:
- The main valuation methods used in India
- How to calculate your EBITDA and apply multiples
- What multiples apply to your industry
- Factors that increase (or decrease) your value
- Common valuation mistakes to avoid

Let's demystify business valuation.

---

## Section 1: Why Valuation Matters

### For Sellers
- **Pricing strategy:** Price too high, you'll sit on the market. Price too low, you leave money on the table.
- **Negotiation anchor:** Your asking price sets the tone for all discussions.
- **Reality check:** Knowing your number helps you decide whether selling makes sense.

### For Buyers
- **Investment decision:** Buyers need to know they'll earn a return.
- **Financing:** Banks require valuations for acquisition loans.
- **Risk assessment:** Valuation reveals strengths and weaknesses.

### The Valuation Gap
There's almost always a gap between what sellers think their business is worth and what buyers will pay. Understanding valuation methodology helps you bridge that gap—or recognize when expectations are unrealistic.

## Section 2: The Three Main Valuation Methods

### Method 1: Earnings Multiple (Most Common)

**How it works:** Multiply your earnings by an industry-specific multiple.

**Formula:**
```
Business Value = Earnings × Multiple
```

**Earnings Metric:** Most commonly EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) or SDE (Seller's Discretionary Earnings) for smaller businesses.

**Example:**
- Annual EBITDA: ₹50 lakhs
- Industry multiple: 3x
- Estimated value: ₹1.5 crores

**When to use:** Best for profitable, established businesses with consistent cash flow.

**Pros:**
- Widely understood by buyers
- Based on actual financial performance
- Comparable across businesses

**Cons:**
- Requires accurate financial records
- Multiples vary significantly by industry and size
- Doesn't capture all value drivers

---

### Method 2: Asset-Based Valuation

**How it works:** Calculate the value of everything your business owns, minus what it owes.

**Formula:**
```
Business Value = Total Assets - Total Liabilities
```

**Types of Asset Valuation:**

**Book Value:** Assets at their recorded accounting value (often understates true value).

**Fair Market Value:** Assets at what they'd sell for today (more accurate but requires appraisals).

**Liquidation Value:** What you'd get in a forced, quick sale (lowest valuation).

**Example:**
| Asset | Book Value | Fair Market Value |
|-------|------------|------------------|
| Equipment | ₹30L | ₹45L |
| Inventory | ₹20L | ₹18L |
| Real Estate | ₹50L | ₹1.2Cr |
| Receivables | ₹15L | ₹12L |
| **Total Assets** | **₹1.15Cr** | **₹1.95Cr** |
| Less: Liabilities | ₹40L | ₹40L |
| **Net Value** | **₹75L** | **₹1.55Cr** |

**When to use:**
- Asset-heavy businesses (manufacturing, real estate)
- Businesses with minimal or negative profits
- Liquidation scenarios

**Pros:**
- Tangible and verifiable
- Provides a floor value
- Useful for asset-heavy industries

**Cons:**
- Ignores earning potential
- Doesn't capture intangible value (brand, relationships)
- Can undervalue profitable businesses

---

### Method 3: Revenue Multiple

**How it works:** Multiply annual revenue by an industry multiple.

**Formula:**
```
Business Value = Annual Revenue × Multiple
```

**Example:**
- Annual revenue: ₹2 crores
- Industry multiple: 0.5x
- Estimated value: ₹1 crore

**When to use:**
- High-growth businesses (even if not yet profitable)
- SaaS and subscription businesses
- Businesses with predictable revenue streams

**Common Revenue Multiples:**
| Business Type | Typical Multiple |
|--------------|------------------|
| SaaS (high growth) | 3-10x ARR |
| E-commerce | 0.5-2x revenue |
| Professional services | 0.5-1x revenue |
| Manufacturing | 0.3-0.7x revenue |

**Pros:**
- Simple to calculate
- Useful for growth companies
- Revenue is harder to manipulate than earnings

**Cons:**
- Ignores profitability
- Multiples vary wildly
- Can overvalue unprofitable businesses

## Section 3: Understanding EBITDA Multiples

### What is EBITDA?

**EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization**

It's a proxy for operating cash flow—what the business generates before financing decisions and non-cash expenses.

### How to Calculate EBITDA

**Start with Net Profit:**
```
Net Profit (from P&L)
+ Interest Expense
+ Tax Expense
+ Depreciation
+ Amortization
= EBITDA
```

**Example Calculation:**
| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Net Profit | ₹30,00,000 |
| + Interest | ₹5,00,000 |
| + Taxes | ₹8,00,000 |
| + Depreciation | ₹7,00,000 |
| + Amortization | ₹0 |
| **EBITDA** | **₹50,00,000** |

### Adjusted EBITDA (What Buyers Actually Use)

Buyers don't just look at reported EBITDA—they adjust for:

**Add-backs (increase EBITDA):**
- Owner's excess salary above market rate
- Personal expenses run through the business
- One-time expenses (legal dispute, relocation)
- Non-recurring costs

**Subtractions (decrease EBITDA):**
- Below-market rent (if owner owns property)
- Related-party transactions at non-market rates
- Unsustainable cost savings

**Example Adjusted EBITDA:**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Reported EBITDA | ₹50,00,000 |
| + Owner salary above market (₹25L vs ₹10L market) | ₹15,00,000 |
| + Owner's car expenses | ₹3,00,000 |
| + One-time legal settlement | ₹5,00,000 |
| - Below-market rent to self | (₹4,00,000) |
| **Adjusted EBITDA** | **₹69,00,000** |

*This is the number that gets multiplied.*

## Section 4: Industry Multiples in India

### EBITDA Multiples by Industry

| Industry | Typical Multiple Range | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| **Manufacturing** | 2.0 - 4.0x | Higher for specialized/IP-protected |
| **Retail (single location)** | 1.5 - 2.5x | Location-dependent |
| **Retail (chain)** | 2.5 - 4.0x | Scale premium |
| **E-commerce** | 2.0 - 5.0x | Higher for strong brands |
| **D2C Brands** | 3.0 - 6.0x | Brand equity matters |
| **IT Services** | 3.0 - 5.0x | Recurring revenue premium |
| **SaaS** | 5.0 - 10.0x | ARR-based, growth-dependent |
| **Distribution** | 2.0 - 3.5x | Territory and contracts matter |
| **Restaurants (single)** | 1.5 - 2.5x | Lease terms critical |
| **Restaurant chains** | 3.0 - 5.0x | Brand and scalability |
| **Healthcare clinics** | 2.5 - 4.0x | Doctor-dependency affects value |
| **Professional services** | 1.5 - 3.0x | Key person risk |
| **Logistics** | 2.5 - 4.0x | Assets and contracts |
| **Education** | 2.0 - 4.0x | Varies by segment |

### What Affects Your Multiple?

**Higher Multiples:**
- Consistent revenue growth (>15% YoY)
- High profit margins
- Recurring revenue
- Diversified customer base
- Strong management team
- Clean financials
- Defensible market position
- Growth potential

**Lower Multiples:**
- Declining or flat revenue
- Customer concentration (>30% from one client)
- Owner dependency
- Messy financials
- Commodity business
- High competition
- Regulatory risk

## Section 5: SDE vs EBITDA (For Smaller Businesses)

### Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)

For businesses under ₹2 crore in earnings, buyers often use **SDE** instead of EBITDA.

**SDE = EBITDA + Owner's Total Compensation**

This represents the total benefit available to a working owner.

**When to Use SDE:**
- Small businesses where owner actively works
- Businesses that will be owner-operated post-sale
- Revenue under ₹5 crores typically

**SDE Calculation:**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| EBITDA | ₹25,00,000 |
| + Owner salary | ₹12,00,000 |
| + Owner benefits | ₹3,00,000 |
| **SDE** | **₹40,00,000** |

**SDE Multiples (Smaller Businesses):**
| SDE Range | Typical Multiple |
|-----------|------------------|
| ₹10-25 lakhs | 1.5 - 2.5x |
| ₹25-50 lakhs | 2.0 - 3.0x |
| ₹50L - 1 crore | 2.5 - 3.5x |
| ₹1-2 crore | 3.0 - 4.0x |

## Section 7: Factors That Increase Business Value

### Financial Factors

1. **Consistent Growth**
   - 3+ years of revenue growth signals sustainability
   - Aim for 15%+ annual growth

2. **Strong Margins**
   - Higher margins = more valuable
   - Document what drives your margins

3. **Recurring Revenue**
   - Subscriptions, retainers, maintenance contracts
   - Can add 1-2x to your multiple

4. **Clean Financials**
   - Audited books command premium
   - Separate business and personal completely

5. **Low Working Capital Needs**
   - Less cash trapped in operations = more attractive

### Operational Factors

6. **Management Team**
   - Business runs without you = higher value
   - Document key processes

7. **Diversified Customers**
   - No single customer >15% of revenue
   - Multiple industries reduces risk

8. **Defensible Position**
   - Patents, exclusive contracts, brand recognition
   - Anything competitors can't easily copy

9. **Growth Runway**
   - Untapped markets, new products, expansion plans
   - Buyers pay for potential

10. **Transferable Relationships**
    - Supplier contracts, customer relationships
    - Written agreements vs handshakes

## Section 8: Common Valuation Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Overvaluing Based on Potential
*"The business could do ₹10 crores if someone just..."*

Buyers pay for proven results, not dreams. Potential adds a modest premium—not 5x your current earnings.

### Mistake 2: Comparing to Startup Valuations
*"That VC-backed company raised at 20x revenue!"*

VC valuations reflect hyper-growth expectations and different economics. Most SMBs don't qualify.

### Mistake 3: Including Personal Assets
*"Plus my car and office furniture..."*

Only business assets that transfer count. And they're often worth less than you think.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Liabilities
*"My equipment is worth ₹1 crore!"*

Minus the ₹40 lakhs equipment loan = ₹60 lakhs in value to buyer.

### Mistake 5: Not Adjusting for Owner Involvement
If you work 80 hours/week and pay yourself ₹50,000/month, a buyer will need to hire someone at market rate. That cost comes off the top.

### Mistake 6: Expecting Same Multiple as Larger Companies
Larger businesses command higher multiples. A ₹50 crore company might sell at 6x EBITDA; a ₹2 crore company at 3x. This is normal.

### Mistake 7: Anchoring on What You "Need"
*"I need ₹3 crores to retire comfortably."*

What you need is irrelevant to value. The market determines price.

## FAQ Section

**Q: What valuation method is best for my business?**
A: Most profitable SMBs are valued using EBITDA or SDE multiples. Asset-based valuation is better for capital-intensive businesses or those with minimal profits. Revenue multiples suit high-growth companies.

**Q: Why is my business valued at only 2-3x profits? That seems low.**
A: Small business multiples reflect risk. Buyers consider owner dependency, customer concentration, and market conditions. Larger, more stable companies command higher multiples.

**Q: Can I increase my business value before selling?**
A: Yes! Improving financials, reducing owner dependency, diversifying customers, and documenting processes can significantly increase your multiple. Ideally, start 1-2 years before selling.

**Q: How accurate are online business valuation calculators?**
A: They provide rough estimates only. Professional valuations consider nuances that calculators miss: industry trends, customer quality, competitive position, and more. Use calculators as a starting point, not a final answer.
